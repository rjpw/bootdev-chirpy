## Notes

It's a couple of weeks since I've made any progress on this project. This is both a problem and an opportunity. The problem: I'm losing the thread. The opportunity: I get to test the architectural breadcrumbs I left myself that can get me back on track.

First order of business: run the tests (they passed). 

Next order of business, get the big picture. In my last session I had gone on a long tangent about architecture, and the goal of the tangent was to "shift left" on QA and operational concerns, hopefully without derailing forward progress on feature delivery.

I definitely spent a lot of energy on problems that weren't part of the course. The question is, was any of that effort worth it.

There's an AI twist to this question, but not in the way one might think (i.e. did AI do the work). I asked the AI for help in constructing a roadmap for the architectural concerns, and then I proceeded to follow that roadmap. Those are some useful breadcrumbs for today's efforts, but they raise a question about agency: did the AI steer me down a productive road, or did it humour me while I steered myself off a productivity cliff?

How can I measure this? Here are some of the wishy-washy things I wanted out of the tangent:

1. A stronger architectural foundation in the codebase itself, one that fosters layered testability and change
2. A mental model of those architectural concerns, one that will give me the tools to improve a future Go codebase in ways that resonate with my previous experience as a developer, and my hopes for a better future
3. A vocabulary for my architectural evaluation, one that will hopefully stand up in different solution domains
4. A level of confidence in Go as a language, and whether or not it fits the domains I'm interested in exploring for the next decade

On this basis, how did I (or we, if you include the AI) do?

1. It does feel more robust, especially from having just run the tests. It also feels like it's more work to take next steps, which isn't great, but TBH I'm okay with there being a governor on the motor of change.
2. This part I feel I'm still working on. I'm going to review the documents and see if they still resonate, and then look at the code to see if I can map mental model into syntax and structure. I'm also going to revisit these docs in the light of some of other recent excursions, in particular the ideas of simplicity and immutability in Rich Hickey's work on Clojure.
3. Again, I feel like I need to review the docs. I'm not firm on the vocabulary.
4. I feel confident that Go can do backend work, and that's my focus right now. I don't know about the ten year vision, but that's partly just my nerves about a paused career. I can't imagine adopting Clojure, for example, though I really love what Hickey is selling.

Verdict: did the AI steer me off a cliff? No. I would definitely have gone down these roads myself, and I do think these questions needed answers. The best way for me to understand a system is to think deeply about it. If all I did was blindly put one foot after another in the course, I wouldn't be doing the learning I need to do.
